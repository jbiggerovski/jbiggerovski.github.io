"Who cares. Nothing matters. Everything sucks." 

Did anything encapsulate 2017 better? In light of the year’s incessant noise, I'm sending along some of 2017's loveliest sounds in hopes of bringing you joy and warmth in the new year. A shedload of new music awaits you below, separated into LP’s, EP’s, and Reissues. Click either the album art to download, or the album title to stream (if available). 

First up: a MIX of everything below. 

Here's to new sounds, thought, and action in 2018. 
 
 
⋅LP⋅

 
 
 
 
 
Syd 
Fin

In a year where the last thing I wanted to hear was the sound of a man's voice, Fin, Sydney Bennet's moonlit debut, served as a kind of aural antidote to 2017’s toxic masculinity.

Good weed, good sex, and good life—the tenets of modern R&B— may form the record’s familiar narrative, but it's Syd's singular POV that transcends the genre's narrow confines. Her airy falsetto and somnambulant delivery makes every line feel like it’s emerging from the smokey corner of a dark club, melting our pants off with purred come-ons and commands. 

Between the earworm hooks, the Aaliyah/Missy/Timbo retroisms, and the shattering of R&B's patriarchial arcehtypes, Fin simply meant the most to me this year. If you've yet to sink into it, I genuinely envy the discovery that awaits you. 


   
 
 
 
 
Kelly Lee Owens 
Kelly Lee Owens 

As our social, political and economic systems continued to reveal their advanced stages of decay, all I wanted this year was a bit of sanctuary. Since Kelly Lee Owens’ eponymous debut emerged in early spring, this record became the rock I’d often cling to in the storm. 

Where au courant pop—see Melodrama and Reputation—tends to stitch multiple moving parts together for dramtic effect, balancing harsh timbres and exulting melodies for maximum spectacle, KLO eschews this structural maximalism in favor of skeletal arrangements and hypnotic repetition. Despite the new age trimmings— okay, yeah, those are Tibetan singing bowls on ”CBM”—Owens achieves an alchemical mixture of perfect pop by combining the emotional resonance of strong songwriting with the rhythmic urgency of house and techno. In striking this harmonious balance, KLO perfects one of dance music's darkest arts: moving your body while activating your heart. OMG, don’t tell Sheba I ever said that. Oy.
 
 
 
 
Sza
Ctrl

At bleeding last Solana Imani Rowe pings off the decade's finest debut with this lavish collection of glittering pearls. Between her serpentine melodies, elastic cadences, and diaristic songwriting, Sza delivered the best vocal performance in recent memory.

It's nigh-on imposs to hear a single note of Rowe's sinuous voice and not fall under her spell. When Sza tells you to drop your drawls on “The Weekend”, a polyamorous slow jam, you damn for sure listen. But it’s the peerless “Prom”— the best song of 2017—where Sza slays the competition with her formal mastery of blissful introspection. 

Only cringes you’ll find here belong to the boys, of course. Never let Travis Scott near your garden, and if anyone ever mistakes you for a clitoris, blame Kung-Fu Kenny. smh

No messing about, Ctrl is an utter classic. 
 
 
 
 
Yaeji
EP's 1+2


Straight up, the most fun I had all year were with Yaeji’s two EP’s, a double dose of jukin’ hip house and smudged dream pop. From the beatless daydream of “Feelings Change” to the bumping swerve of “Feel It Out”, Yaeji established herself as the year's most promising pop star in just eight short months. Peep the "Drink I'm Sippin On" video for proof, where a stuntin’ Yaeji and her adorable squad make NYC seem cool for the first time in a decade. Drop the needle and get flexed on. Shit is Yaeji. 
 
 
 
 
Hypnotic Brass Band 
Book of Sound

Prepare to get lost in the radiant joy of Book of Sound, a bittersweet farewell to Phil Cohran from the Hypnotic Brass Band, the group comprised of Cohran’s seven sons. HBB carries the torch of Cohran's legacy with this mournful meditation on the group’s earliest beginnings as the Wolf Pack, an era spent playing the streets of Chicago without a drummer. Cohran and Chicago emerge as the record northern star, guiding the group to stand on their own as some of the finest musicians in the city. Cohran would beam over this. 
 
 
 
 
DJ Python
Dulce Compañia 

Even if “I feel like I’m in a sushi restaurant,” were the first words out of Sheba’s derisive mouth when she initally heard Dulce Compañia, Brian Pinéro’s delicious debut of super-swung reggaeton and amorous house, she never stopped moving while it was on. Her clenched fists swung with those shifting shoulders, which she'd counter with the flick of her tiny hips. Oh, and those pursed lips—my god stop fronting! Don’t care if it’s you or Sheba, no one can resist that bump. 
 
 
 
 
Mica Levi 
Delete Beach  

Demigod Mica Levi cements her GOAT status with this dank AF soundtrack for Delete Beach, the award-winning animé short by Phil Collins—naw, different British dude. Operating in pure greyscale, Levi crafts a hypnotic, harrowing trance you’d give anything to escape if it weren’t so intoxicating. Imagine the opening skit of Gza’s Liquid Swords stretched into twenty-seven breathless minutes of eerie dissonance, with charred layers of strings and rickety electronic unraveling their mysteries upon every spin. With its meditation on humanity’s progress and decline, Delete Beach became the perfect soundtrack for these uncertain times.
 
 
 
 
Equiknoxx
Colón Man

Supremely daring transmissions from two of Kingston’s best. Gavsborg and Time Cow continue their imperial flex on dancehall with Colón Man, a psychotropic potage of rugged beats and batshit samples. Their debut proper finds the duo scouring esoteric sources for the most unusual timbres and textures, all of which they tether to the tropical bounce of 3/2 rhythms. If initial spins seem a little clinical, just be patient: Colón Man reveals its infinite gifts with repeated listens. I just wanna hug this record. 
 
 
 
 
Priests
Nothing Feels Natural

Far and away the Sheebiest record on this list. For real, you’d be hard fucking pressed to find a musical reference post-Reagan’s second term on Nothing Feels Natural, the DC Post-Punkers’s enigmatic debut. From ESG to Siouxisie to X-Ray Spex, Priests ticks virtually every box on Sheebie’s wish list

Excluding linen sheets—Sheba adores them; I find they feel like burlap and don’t like being exfoliated in my sleep—music is our primary source of disagreement. She maintains a flawless record collection, but that shit has more angry white dudes in it than Trump’s cabinet. So you can imagine my excitement the first time I played her Nothing Feels Natural and she asked "Is this new?" I nearly plotzed. Finally! I had found a record we could both adore. So, in that spirit, please accept this record as a gift, from our feuding house to yours. 

Love, 
Sheba and John
